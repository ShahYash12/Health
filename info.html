What is Brain Stroke

A brain stroke, also known as a cerebrovascular accident (CVA), occurs when there is a disruption of blood supply to a part of the brain, leading to damage or death of brain cells. This disruption of blood supply can be caused by a blockage in the blood vessels that supply the brain (ischemic stroke) or by bleeding in the brain (hemorrhagic stroke). When brain cells do not receive the oxygen and nutrients they need due to the disrupted blood supply, they start to die. This can lead to various symptoms depending on the area of the brain affected, such as paralysis or weakness of one side of the body, difficulty speaking or understanding speech, loss of vision or double vision, dizziness, and severe headache. It is important to seek immediate medical attention if you or someone you know is experiencing any symptoms of a stroke, as early treatment can significantly improve the outcome. Some risk factors for stroke include high blood pressure, smoking, diabetes, and heart disease, and lifestyle modifications and medications can help reduce the risk of stroke.



Preventive Measures for Brain Stroke

1. Lower Blood Presure

High blood pressure is a huge factor, doubling or even quadrupling your stroke risk if it is not controlled. High blood pressure is the biggest contributor to the risk of stroke in both men and women. Monitoring blood pressure and, if it is elevated, treating it, is probably the biggest difference people can make to their vascular health. Your goal: An ideal goal is maintaining a blood pressure of less than 120/80. But there may be good reasons why you and your doctor will not want your readings to be this low. For some, a less aggressive goal (such as no higher than 140/90) may be more appropriate.
